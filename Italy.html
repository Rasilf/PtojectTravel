IIIIIIIIIIItaly — A Travel Reflection

By : Rasil Faisal
6 May 2026


A personal experience

I don’t really want to pretend I’ve “been everywhere,” but Italy has always felt like one of those places you already know before you even step into it.
Like you’ve seen it in films, heard it in songs, and somehow built a version of it in your mind long before booking anything real.

I remember arriving with this mix of curiosity and expectation like I already knew parts of it, but I wanted to see what would feel different in real life.
And honestly, it doesn’t disappoint. Everything feels layered. Not in a dramatic way, just… full of history, but also very present at the same time.


- Rome

Rome hit me like that the most. It’s not just ruins or famous spots, it’s how the past and present sit right next to each other without trying to compete.
You’re walking past something ancient, then two seconds later someone is grabbing coffee like it’s the most normal thing in the world.
And what surprised me more is how that same contrast shows up in a completely different way too fashion. Italy doesn’t feel stuck in history at all.
It feels like it uses its past as a base and then builds something modern on top of it. You see it in the people, the style, the way even simple outfits feel intentional.
It’s like Rome can hold centuries of history and still somehow feel connected to Milan’s fashion energy at the same time.
That balance between “old world” and “modern identity” is what makes it feel alive in a way that doesn’t feel staged.




- Trevi Fountain

One of the places that stayed in my mind the most was the Trevi Fountain.
I had seen it so many times in movies and photos before, but standing there in real life felt completely different.

What I liked most wasn’t just the fountain itself, but the small tradition connected to it, people throw a coin while making a wish .
It sounds simple, but when you’re actually there, you understand why people do it. There’s something strangely emotional about the moment.


The tradition is to stand with your back facing the fountain and throw a coin over your shoulder while making a wish.



It reminded me of childhood and all the little myths I used to believe in.
Like putting my broken tooth under the pillow and waking up the next morning to find it had turned into money.
Or the fairy my parents convinced me was real, who came to my window every morning to leave my allowance, because it was her way of showing how excited she was for me to go to school.
Or the idea that stars were actually the souls of people we loved, watching us from the sky.

Back in Rome, I remember thinking about how many people stood in that exact same place before me, making wishes, hoping for something, or just wanting to return to Rome one day.




- Food

The food… I don’t think I fully understood Italian food until I had it there. It’s simple, but it doesn’t feel basic.
It feels like someone cared enough not to overcomplicate it. Pasta, bread, coffee , nothing is trying to impress you, but somehow everything does.

And it wasn’t just one place. I remember small trattorias where the menu wasn’t even complicated, just handwritten dishes that change slightly depending on the day.
In one place I had a very simple carbonara that honestly didn’t need anything else with it.
It was creamy, balanced, nothing extra , just perfect in its simplicity.
In another, a pizza that was so light it didn’t feel like the usual heavy version you get elsewhere, more like something you could eat and still feel good after.
Even the coffee culture surprised me standing at the counter, quick espresso, no drama, just part of the day, almost like a habit everyone shares without even thinking about it.

If I had to put it in a small list of what stayed with me from the food side, it would be something like:

  • Fresh pasta
  • Simple pizzas
  • Espresso
  • Tiramisu




- Turin

Turin was one of the cities I was really excited to visit.
I already had an interest in it because of football, especially Juventus -My favorite team-, so it felt like a place I wanted to see in real life, not just on screen or in matches.
That connection made me look forward to the city even more before arriving.

When I got there, I spent time just walking around the city center and exploring it in a simple way.
Turin felt calm and organized, with wide streets and a mix of older buildings and modern shops. It wasn’t overwhelming or crowded, which made it easy to just walk without a specific plan.
I also stopped by a small local souvenir shop, where I looked around and bought a few small items as memories from the trip.
The shop was simple and local, not fancy, but it gave a nice feel of the city.

In the end, Italy for me isn’t about ticking places off a list.
It’s more like a mix of small moments I experienced while I was there walking through cities, trying local food, and just observing how everyday life feels in a different place.
It’s not something I feel like I’ve “finished,” but more like something I’ve experienced a part of, and still want to see more of later.
